AI Coding Tools: My Honest Take After Going From Skeptic to Daily User

Three weeks ago, I would have told you AI coding tools were overhyped junk. I would have said they weren’t meaningfully changing anything and wouldn’t for a long time. Then something shifted, and now I use them daily — heavily. It happened fast enough that I’m still processing it, which is part of why I wanted to write this down.

I’ve been building software for over fourteen years. I’ve watched plenty of technology cycles come and go, and my default posture toward hype is skepticism. So when I say something has genuinely changed, I want to be precise about what that means — and what it doesn’t.

What Changed (And What Didn’t)

My actual workflow hasn’t changed that much. What has changed is my capacity. The AI is doing a lot of work at my direction, but I find myself with more work than ever, not less — because I’m now taking on things I had written off. Side projects I assumed I’d never get to. Domains where the upfront learning cost felt prohibitive. I’m actively working on those now. AI gives me the ability to spin up a project quickly, get a solid foundation in place, and understand how things work at a high level far faster than if I had to do all the research myself.

That said, you can’t blindly trust what it gives you. There are absolutely situations where it produces the most inexplicable solutions — and if you let it lull you into complacency, you’re going to have a bad time.

Spotting When It’s Gone Off the Rails

The heuristic I’ve settled on for detecting AI output that’s gone wrong is, funnily enough, the same one I use for human code that’s gone wrong: spaghetti. If I look at a change set and see logic scattered everywhere, magic numbers, deeply nested conditionals — that’s usually a sign something has gone awry. I need to either implement it myself, provide more context, or rewrite my prompt.

What helps enormously is giving the AI something concrete to work from. Pointing it to another file and saying “use this as a reference” or “treat this as a template” produces dramatically better results. I don’t typically vibe code. I try to plan ahead and give the AI some kind of relevant structure to work within.

Maintainability Still Matters

The reason that approach works is the same reason clean code has always mattered: readability and structure are inputs, not just outputs. If I want the AI to produce clean code, I need to show it clean code. That virtuous cycle reinforces something I’ve always believed — maintainability isn’t optional, even when you can technically get across the goal line without it.

I think of it like the difference between cheap products and well-made ones. Sometimes cheap gets the job done. Other times you need the craftsman. AI can be that craftsman with the right guidance, but it’s unlikely to get there on its own in its current state.

The Real Learning Value

What’s been equally surprising and thrilling is that I’m learning things almost daily. New approaches, new libraries, new stacks, occasionally new languages. The biggest value AI has delivered to me so far might not be its ability to write code, but rather its ability to surface what I don’t know I don’t know — unknowns, ya know? That’s hard to quantify, but genuinely significant. The lowered cost of trying multiple approaches rapidly is part of it too. Rapid iteration teaches you things that slow iteration doesn’t.

On Junior vs. Senior Labels

People often ask whether AI helps junior engineers more or senior engineers more. My honest answer is that I wish we’d stop thinking in those terms. In practice, “senior engineer” usually means someone with a lot of organization-specific domain knowledge — not necessarily someone with exceptional engineering skill. Sometimes that's the case. Sometimes it isn't. The framing people imagine when they use these labels rarely matches reality. AI has the ability to assist any developer. People with less experience may get more lift from it, but I don’t think the junior/senior lens is the useful one here.

What concerns me more is a trend I can already see taking shape: companies using AI productivity as a justification for deprioritizing or outright ignoring early-career engineers in hiring. The logic goes something like — if a senior engineer with AI can do the work of three, why hire someone who’s still learning?

That logic is shortsighted in a way that will hurt the industry long term. Most "senior" engineers were "juniors" once, learning on real codebases, making mistakes in real systems, getting mentored by people who had done it before. That pipeline doesn’t sustain itself. If the industry stops bringing in early-career people because AI makes it feel economically convenient to do so, we’re quietly hollowing out the next generation of experienced engineers. The “seniors” who are so valuable today didn’t emerge from nowhere. Someone made room for them.

I’ll admit something I used to believe privately: that some people just had it and some didn’t. Some innate capacity for this work that either existed or wasn’t going to be coaxed out. I’ve watched enough careers unfold over the past decade to be definitively proven wrong about that. People grow in ways that are genuinely surprising if you give them the time and the environment to do it.

Where Legitimate Skepticism Lives

The real danger isn’t AI becoming smarter than us. It’s us giving it more trust than it deserves. The speed and effortlessness of AI-assisted development creates a very real risk of complacency — of signing off on things you haven’t actually understood.

Its a bit alarmist, but I don't like when AI is described as “thinking” or “understanding,” because I think that framing is inaccurate and makes the trust problem more likely. It anthropomorphizes something that doesn’t warrant that kind of trust. Better vocabulary might not exist. I don’t have any good suggestions here, but I feel like the framing matters.

Ownership and Craft

Do I feel like I’m cheating sometimes? Yes, honestly. Projects move fast enough that it can feel that way. But engineering decisions still matter. Tech stack choices still matter. AI gets genuinely bogged down in poorly-structured codebases, convoluted requests, and excessive configuration.

What I’ve noticed is that I’m investing more care in user experience than before. AI has surpassed me in raw UI design ability, and I’m fine with that — but direction still has to come from me. “Taste” I think is what the kids are calling it these days. Steering the experience, knowing what “right” looks like: that’s still a human job.

Sometimes it can feel like you may not "own" what you're shipping, but I'll say this: If you signed off on it, you best be ready to own it.

The One Thing I’d Tell a Skeptic

Give it a serious try. I was the biggest skeptic three weeks ago. Something has happened in that window of time, and I don’t think I can fully explain it. What I can tell you is that I’m doing more work and having more fun than I have in a while. It almost becomes a game — seeing how much you can get done, how many things you can move forward at once. AI has enhanced my workload, not replaced it. There are more paths available today than were feasible yesterday. That’s worth something.

The discourse around AI tends toward extremes. I’d encourage more nuance — not uncritical enthusiasm, but not reflexive dismissal either. What’s actually happening is more interesting than either camp gives it credit for.
