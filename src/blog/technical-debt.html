Technical Debt Is Real Debt — Treat It That Way

Technical debt is one of those terms that gets thrown around quite a bit — so much so that it has become malleable and unclear. Spend enough time in the industry and you’ll hear it used as a battle cry, a shield, and an excuse — sometimes all in the same standup. Over the years, I’ve developed a few opinions on what it actually is, what it isn’t, and how to keep it from quietly destroying a codebase.

It Really Does Behave Like Debt

The financial analogy holds up pretty well. You can drown in technical debt. If you’ve ever maxed out a credit card and found yourself making minimum payments just to keep the lights on, you know the feeling — that low-grade dread of a hole that keeps getting deeper while you’re just trying to stay afloat.

Technical debt is generally created in the name of speed. You mock an API that doesn’t exist yet. You implement the happy path and skip the edge cases for now. You ship a proof-of-concept to learn something fast. These are all legitimate. They share a common thread: there’s a reason, it’s understood, and the shortcut is in service of a goal.

This is all well and good, but if you’re just doing the bare amount of work needed to keep the system running — if you’re just making minimum payments — you’re one small disaster away from bankruptcy. Debt has to be paid down regularly — in chunks, faster than it accumulates. You can’t work out once a month and expect to be in shape. It has to be a habit.

There’s a Fake Version, and It’s a Trap

Here’s where things get uncomfortable. There’s another kind of technical debt: the kind that gets the label applied after the fact, retroactively, to cover up code that was just written badly.

Spaghetti everywhere? Oh, it’s just technical debt. We’ll pay that down later. You won’t. And you probably knew that when you said it.

The distinction usually comes down to readability. Legitimate debt — the intentional kind — can still be clean and easy to understand. It’s incomplete by design, not incomprehensible by neglect. When reviewing code or inheriting a codebase, that’s a good thing to look for. Messy by nature, or messy because someone didn’t want to deal with the pain of doing it right? If it’s the latter, calling it technical debt is just putting a respectable name on something that isn’t.

Deciding When to Take It On

A question to ask: does this move the ball in a meaningful way?

You may not be throwing the bomb downfield, but is it at least a checkdown in the flat? Does it gain yardage? The debt you take on should be something you can build on. If you can see that the next step is going to require undoing what you just did, that’s probably not the right trade. Three-yard gains followed by ten-yard sacks don’t win the division.

The proof-of-concept is a special case. Those projects are often designed to be thrown away, and that’s fine — throwing them away isn’t moving backwards if they did their job. The goal was to learn something, and if you learned it, the POC succeeded regardless of whether the code survives.

Stop Asking Permission to Do Your Job

Here’s the take that tends to get some pushback: I don’t think debt repayment should be a line item you negotiate with management.

The whole framing of “making the case for refactoring to stakeholders” assumes that paying down technical debt is optional work that competes with real work. It isn’t. It’s part of doing the job properly. When you ship a package, you don’t negotiate with the carrier about how many stops the truck makes. Those logistics are part of the shipping process — they just happen. Technical debt repayment should work the same way.

This is largely a developer-created problem. We’ve spent so long feeling the need to justify our time in terms of feature points and deliverables that we’ve trained everyone around us to think that code health is a special project rather than a basic professional responsibility. And honestly? I think part of it is that we can’t stop explaining ourselves. Cataloguing every step we took, every tradeoff we made — it can become a habit of looking busy rather than providing real value.

Management knowing that technical debt exists is often a symptom of that habit. If we’re doing our job well, they shouldn’t need to know.

The Real Answer Is Discipline

The most valuable habit for keeping debt under control is the one that’s hardest to develop: discipline. Such is the case with many things in life.

Think before you type. Be intentional about what you’re doing and why. Make the good decisions upfront when they’re cheap, rather than later when they’re expensive. And take your medicine — pay down debt regularly, before it eats you.

It’s a simple answer. It’s not an easy one. But it’s the real one.
