The Trap of “Correct” Architecture

For a long time, I believed there was a right way to build software. Not just a good way — the right way. I read everything I could get my hands on: books on domain-driven design, papers on distributed systems, blog posts about CQRS and event sourcing and the latest patterns the industry was championing. I wanted to master the craft, and I was convinced that mastery meant finding the foolproof, sure-fire architecture for any problem.

For a while, it seemed to work. My code was reliable, backed extensively by tests, and read beautifully. My work was slow, but I told myself that was just the price of doing things right.

Then the cracks started to show.

When Collaboration Breaks Down

The first warning sign was collaboration. Whenever I brought someone else onto a project, I had to teach them my system first. And it never quite worked. Whatever they contributed didn’t fit the way I’d envisioned it, and I’d quietly go back and fix it myself. I told myself this was a people problem. It wasn’t.

The second warning sign was momentum. Projects I poured myself into had a tendency to stagnate. Meanwhile, I kept looking back at earlier work — simpler work — that people actually used and loved. What was different about those?

The answer was straightforward: they solved problems. They were true tools. Pragmatic. No ceremony, no architecture for architecture’s sake. Just software that did what it needed to do.

I had let my desire to be seen as a craftsman lead me somewhere counterproductive — into over-engineering, into solutions that begged for a problem to fit into rather than the other way around.

What Pragmatism Actually Looks Like

The shift changed how I work with other people as much as it changed how I write code. When a solution is grounded in a problem everyone already understands, it’s much easier to talk about, reason about, and work on together. You don’t have to teach someone a special, non-obvious system before they can contribute. The solution makes itself obvious.

The trap is that it’s easy to convince yourself your complex solution is easy to understand — especially when you’re the one at the center of it. Developing the ability to see and prevent that trap is important.

If you find yourself editing thirty-plus files to add a feature, something has probably gone wrong upstream. Good architecture makes changes local. Complexity that spreads everywhere is complexity that will hurt you.

Experience Is the Real Teacher

None of this is something you can fully learn from a book, including this post. Experience is the great teacher — it does more for you than any other form of learning, likely more than all others combined.

The best way to get that experience is to build things you actually use. Make your own todo app. I know — everyone builds that and thinks their version has the special sauce, but it does give you something valuable: a direct feedback loop between you as the builder and you as the user. You build a feature, live with it for a bit, and then think, you know what would be really cool… That’s iteration. That’s what agile is actually supposed to be.

You also end up with software tailored to your own workflow, which I can’t stress the value of enough. See it through and you will be more productive in your everyday and likely will find motivation in the process.

Two Cents

If I could go back and tell my earlier self one thing, it would be this: build. Always be building. Prioritize it above all other things.

Building gives you motivation. Finishing gives you something to show. Identify a need you have — something real, something yours — and build the thing that solves it. Then repeat.

It’s all in the doing. Be an engineer. Be a problem-solver.
